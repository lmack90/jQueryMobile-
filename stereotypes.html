Stereotypes

Let’s talk about stereotypes.

(I know. It can be an awkward conversation.)

We’ve all heard some stereotypes that made us cringe. The problem with stereotypes is that whether we realize it or not, they can be automatic and unconscious, influencing the decisions we make about other people. And if we belong to a group that’s been harmed by stereotypes, we might alter our own behaviour to make sure those stereotypes don’t work against us.

    What is Performance Bias?

    Performance bias clouds our perception of others’ competence and abilities. We unconsciously assume that certain groups have inherent value, while other groups need to prove that value to us. This means that professionals from under-represented groups may not be given the same opportunities as their peers when it comes to hiring, promotions, and leadership roles. Often, these same professionals are held to stricter or higher standards than those from majority groups. This means we’re losing great talent before we even know it’s there.

    When asked to grade a poorly written legal brief authored by a law student, lawyers gave a significantly higher score if they assumed the student was white. In addition, when the hypothetical student was identified as white, written comments were much more supportive (“needs to focus but has a lot of potential”), than when the student was identified as black (i.e., “I can’t believe he went to NYU.”)

    How do we bust this bias?

      Here are three tangible things we can do to stand up to this bias:

    1. Make a list of objective standards for hiring, promoting, and offering opportunities.
    2. Make sure that list is widely communicated to the rest of the company so it can inform everyone’s decision-making.
    3. Set up evaluations for hiring that are gender- and race-blind.
